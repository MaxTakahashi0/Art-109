Turning Into Noise

Watch on YouTube

Turning into Noise is about the fragments that are lost through digital processes, whether they be fragments of information

The original photo dataset utilized for the video includes photos of myself, my family, and notable photos spanning about 6 years. Merely by choosing these images, I am parsing the data that exists about myself into a collage of “best moments” which immediately eliminates the possibility of the dataset representing entire years of my life. However, the tensors derived from this dataset were able to assist the generation of “a person that is me”. For the purposes of this project I treated each iteration or result as a new version of myself. It felt like honoring a part of myself that has never happened, but is somehow before me.

The spoken words derive from my own, which I wrote to myself 6 years ago. Processing these words distorted them until I no longer recognized or understood the results to be my own writing. My words morphed into nonsense, but I treated the nonsense as if it meant something, because in my mind, the words still belonged to me.

This piece discusses where the line is drawn between self, representation of self, representation of something else, and representation of nothing at all. It tackles the idea that individuals cannot truly be embodied within a program or experience, no matter how extensive or accurate. When the images and words become nothing more than noise, what really matters?

This work also expresses the data that is lost when sources of information are simplified and expounded upon again and again, until there is little information left, and the rest is filler. This speaks to the supposed vastness of the internet in which information is simplified to such small amounts that when we absorb it, we can only recite so much of it in the end. So, as data becomes more openly available, and we ourselves are being turned into data, we too are lost in the noise of the internet. Simplified and overprocessed until we exist as tensors to inform the collection of more data. However, as Hito Seteryl informs us; if this extraction and generalization of ourselves is inevitable, we might as well accept our data as it is and have fun with it.

As human beings, our existence is more than what we put on the internet. It is more than what we tell others, and it is more than what we tell ourselves. Our existences are their own summary, and any simpler of a description is false. But it is tempting to ask yourself, what would I look like in my simplest form?
